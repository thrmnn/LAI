Urban trees are a primary countermeasure to heat island effects, yet quantifying canopy density---Leaf Area Index (LAI)---at city scale remains a critical barrier for evidence-based planning. Traditional inventories are labor-intensive and systematically exclude private-property trees, covering less than half the urban forest. We present an automated framework integrating LiDAR point clouds (25~pts/m²) and high-resolution aerial imagery (0.25m RGB+NIR) with pre-trained deep learning crown segmentation and voxelization-based LAI estimation. A machine learning model predicts per-tree LAI from morphological parameters and genus, enabling deployment in areas with limited LiDAR coverage. Applied to Amsterdam's 12,350 trees, the model achieves R²~=~0.784---a 39\% improvement over allometric baselines---while detecting three times more trees than the municipal database. This scalable framework equips municipalities with city-wide LAI maps for targeted heat-mitigation planning that extends beyond public land.
