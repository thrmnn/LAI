\section{Introduction}

Global mean temperature exceeded 1.5°C above pre-industrial levels in 2024, reaching the Paris Climate Agreement's lower bound \cite{worldmeteorologicalorganizationWMOConfirms20242025,unitednationsParisAgreement2016}. This warming particularly affects urban areas, where over half the world's population resides \cite{SecretaryGeneralReport2023} and where the Urban Heat Island (UHI) effect elevates temperatures 2-5°C above surrounding areas \cite{okeUrbanClimates2017a,mohajeraniUrbanHeatIsland2017a,okeEnergeticBasisUrban1982a,santamourisAnalyzingHeatIsland2015a}. UHI increases heat-related mortality \cite{heavisideUrbanHeatIsland2017a,watts2018ReportLancet2018,faurieAssociationHighTemperature2022} and reduces productivity by 2-4\% per degree above comfort thresholds \cite{kjellstromHeatHumanPerformance2016,speakInfluenceTreeTraits2020,rahmanTreeCoolingEffects2020}. Urban trees effectively counter UHI by reducing ambient temperatures 0.5-2.0°C through altered energy balance \cite{rahmanTraitsTreesCooling2020a,liCoolingEfficacyTrees2024} and providing shade that decreases mean radiant temperatures by up to 20°C \cite{duFieldAssessmentNeighboring2020,middelImpactShadeOutdoor2016,liCoolingEfficacyTrees2024}.

Traditional urban forest management relies on manual surveys by trained arborists, requiring 20-30 minutes per tree \cite{nielsenReviewUrbanTree2014} and yielding infrequent updates with limited spatial coverage. Even comprehensive municipal databases often document only public trees, leaving the majority of urban forests uncharted. Tree attributes are frequently recorded in broad ranges rather than precise measurements, limiting utility for climate modeling. Incomplete tree inventories can underestimate ecosystem services by 15-35\% \cite{nowakUSUrbanForest2018a,thomasTreesOutsideForests2021,przewoznaGeoQuestionnaireEnvironmentalPlanning2021,konijnendijkDefiningUrbanForestry2006}, highlighting the need for improved assessment methods as urban areas expand.
 
To address these limitations, we incorporate individual-tree Leaf Area Index (LAI) assessment. LAI quantifies canopy density as total one-sided leaf area per unit ground surface area (m²/m²) \cite{chenDefiningLeafArea1992}, providing critical insights into light interception capacity. Trees with LAI >3 reduce surface temperatures 8-12°C compared to 3-5°C for lower LAI values \cite{shashua-barMicroclimateModellingStreet2010}. LAI directly quantifies shading potential, photosynthetic capacity, and carbon sequestration \cite{reichKeyCanopyTraits2012,hardimanCouplingFineScaleRoot2017}, while also correlating with air quality improvement, stormwater management, and habitat quality \cite{nowakTreeForestEffects2014,berlandRoleTreesUrban2017a,threlfallApproachesUrbanVegetation2016}. Integrating LAI estimation enables comprehensive assessment of urban tree ecosystem services.

This paper proposes an automated approach for urban forestry management that addresses the limitations of traditional inventory methods. Our methodology employs a voxelization technique (a three-dimensional discretization of space into volumetric pixels) for LAI estimation and leverages deep learning segmentation models to process and analyze three complementary aerial data sources: aerial LiDAR scans with a density of 25 points/m², high-resolution (25cm) RGB orthophotos, and near-infrared imagery. We integrate these data sources to detect climate-relevant morphological features at an individual tree level with significantly higher precision than conventional methods. The approach focuses on extracting key parameters such as tree height, canopy diameter, and LAI, which previous research has identified as the most crucial variables for estimating shading potential and overall ecosystem services \cite{rahmanTraitsTreesCooling2020a,threlfallApproachesUrbanVegetation2016}. The final component of our methodology is a machine learning regression model that predicts LAI from morphological data and taxonomic classification, enabling the scaling of our approach to regions where high-resolution LiDAR data may not be available.

This research enhances urban forest management by automating labor-intensive field measurements, reducing costs through remote sensing, and creating standardized methodologies accessible to municipalities with varying technical capacities. Automating the inventory process enables comprehensive assessment of entire urban forests, including private property trees often excluded from manual surveys. Integrating LAI mapping provides quantitative shade assessment for strategic tree planting in heat-vulnerable areas. As cities face intensifying thermal challenges, this approach bridges remote sensing technology with evidence-based urban planning decisions.
