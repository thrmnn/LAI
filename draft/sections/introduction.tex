\section{Introduction}

With 2024 marking the warmest year on record, global temperatures are trending toward the Paris Climate Agreement's 1.5°C threshold \cite{worldmeteorologicalorganizationWMOConfirms20242025,unitednationsParisAgreement2016}. This warming particularly affects urban areas, where over half the world's population resides \cite{SecretaryGeneralReport2023} and where the Urban Heat Island (UHI) effect elevates temperatures 2--5°C above surrounding areas \cite{okeUrbanClimates2017a,mohajeraniUrbanHeatIsland2017a}, increasing heat-related mortality \cite{heavisideUrbanHeatIsland2017a,watts2018ReportLancet2018} and reducing productivity \cite{kjellstromHeatHumanPerformance2016}. Urban trees effectively counter UHI by reducing ambient temperatures 0.5--2.0°C \cite{rahmanTraitsTreesCooling2020a,liCoolingEfficacyTrees2024} and providing shade that decreases mean radiant temperatures by up to 20°C \cite{duFieldAssessmentNeighboring2020,middelImpactShadeOutdoor2016}.

Traditional urban forest management relies on manual surveys by trained arborists, requiring 20--30 minutes per tree \cite{nielsenReviewUrbanTree2014} and yielding infrequent updates with limited spatial coverage. Municipal databases typically document only public trees, leaving the majority of urban forests---often on private property---uncharted. Automated remote sensing approaches offer scalability, but LiDAR-based canopy density estimation requires high-density point clouds (>20~pts/m²) that remain unavailable in many municipalities, limiting applicability. Incomplete inventories can underestimate ecosystem services by 15--35\% \cite{nowakUSUrbanForest2018a,thomasTreesOutsideForests2021}, highlighting the need for scalable, adaptable assessment methods.

Leaf Area Index (LAI)---total one-sided leaf area per unit ground area (m²/m²)---quantifies canopy density and directly determines shading potential \cite{chenDefiningLeafArea1992}. Trees with LAI >3 reduce surface temperatures 8--12°C compared to 3--5°C for lower LAI values \cite{shashua-barMicroclimateModellingStreet2010}. LAI also correlates with photosynthetic capacity, carbon sequestration, air quality improvement, and stormwater management \cite{reichKeyCanopyTraits2012,nowakTreeForestEffects2014,threlfallApproachesUrbanVegetation2016}, making it a critical parameter for comprehensive urban ecosystem service assessment.

This paper presents an automated framework for urban tree inventory with individual-tree LAI estimation. Our methodology integrates high-resolution aerial imagery (25cm RGB and near-infrared) with LiDAR point clouds, employing deep learning for crown segmentation and voxelization for LAI estimation. Critically, the hybrid approach is adaptive to variable point densities (5--20~pts/m²), enabling deployment in cities where high-density LiDAR remains unavailable. A machine learning regression model then predicts LAI from morphological parameters and taxonomic classification. Applied to Amsterdam, this approach detects three times more trees than municipal databases while achieving R²~=~0.784---a 39\% improvement over traditional allometric methods.

This research enables comprehensive assessment of entire urban forests, including private property trees often excluded from manual surveys, providing quantitative shade assessment for strategic tree planting in heat-vulnerable areas. The methodology offers municipalities an automated, cost-effective tool for evidence-based urban heat mitigation planning as cities face intensifying thermal challenges under climate change.
