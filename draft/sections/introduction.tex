\section{Introduction}

With 2024 marking the warmest year on record, global temperatures are trending toward the Paris Climate Agreement's 1.5°C threshold \cite{worldmeteorologicalorganizationWMOConfirms20242025,unitednationsParisAgreement2016}. This warming particularly affects urban areas, where over half the world's population resides \cite{unitednationsWorldUrbanization2018} and where the Urban Heat Island (UHI) effect elevates temperatures 2--5°C above surrounding areas \cite{okeUrbanClimates2017a,mohajeraniUrbanHeatIsland2017a}, increasing heat-related mortality \cite{heavisideUrbanHeatIsland2017a,watts2018ReportLancet2018} and reducing productivity \cite{kjellstromHeatHumanPerformance2016}. Urban trees effectively counter UHI by reducing ambient temperatures 0.5--2.0°C \cite{rahmanTraitsTreesCooling2020a,liCoolingEfficacyTrees2024} and providing shade that decreases mean radiant temperatures by up to 20°C \cite{duFieldAssessmentNeighboring2020,middelImpactShadeOutdoor2016}.

Traditional urban forest management relies on manual surveys by trained arborists, requiring 20--30 minutes per tree \cite{nielsenReviewUrbanTree2014} and yielding infrequent updates with limited spatial coverage. Municipal databases typically document only public trees, leaving the majority of urban forests---often on private property---uncharted. Automated remote sensing approaches offer scalability, but LiDAR-based canopy density estimation requires high-density point clouds (>20~pts/m²) that remain unavailable in many municipalities, limiting applicability. Incomplete inventories can underestimate ecosystem services by 15--35\% \cite{nowakUSUrbanForest2018a,thomasTreesOutsideForests2021}, highlighting the need for scalable, adaptable assessment methods.

Among biophysical indicators, Leaf Area Index (LAI)---defined as total one-sided leaf area per unit ground area (m²/m²) \cite{chenDefiningLeafArea1992}---is uniquely suited for heat-mitigation assessment because it links canopy structure directly to radiative shielding. High-LAI canopies (>3) lower surface temperatures 8--12°C, roughly triple the reduction observed under sparser crowns \cite{shashua-barMicroclimateModellingStreet2010}. Beyond shading, LAI governs photosynthetic capacity, carbon sequestration, air-quality improvement, and stormwater interception \cite{reichKeyCanopyTraits2012,nowakTreeForestEffects2014,threlfallApproachesUrbanVegetation2016}, positioning it as a single metric that captures multiple urban ecosystem services simultaneously.

To overcome these limitations, we develop a multi-source remote sensing pipeline that pairs 25cm four-band aerial imagery with airborne LiDAR, using deep learning--based crown delineation and a voxel extinction model for individual-tree LAI retrieval (Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline}). This core framework detects trees---including those on private land---and estimates their canopy density directly from three-dimensional point-cloud structure, adapting to variable point densities (5--20~pts/m²). To extend LAI mapping beyond areas with LiDAR coverage, a supervised regression model is trained on the LiDAR-derived estimates and predicts per-tree LAI from morphological parameters and genus alone. Evaluated across Amsterdam, the framework detects three times more trees than official registries, while the prediction model achieves R²~=~0.784 on held-out data.

By capturing trees on private land---routinely omitted from arborist-led inventories---the resulting city-wide LAI maps give planners a spatially explicit basis for prioritising shade interventions in heat-vulnerable neighbourhoods. The approach thus converts fragmented canopy data into an actionable decision layer for climate-adaptive urban forestry.
